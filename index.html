"The Death of Samir" ‚Äì The Morning After

Narrated by Mohamed

I didn't sleep. None of us did. The wind howled all night, trying to erase what happened. By dawn, the sand had already begun to cover the small mound where we buried Samir. Too quick. Too easy. Like the desert wanted to swallow the memory as well as the man.

Ali sat a few steps from the grave, knees to his chest, staring at nothing. I sat beside him.

"You blame me," I said.

He didn't look up. "Do you blame yourself?"

"Yes," I said. "Every step."

The wind shifted. Khalifa and Massoud's faint argument over the last date floated by, then died.

"You could've ordered him back," Ali said finally.

"I did," I said. "But we're not soldiers. We're boys with no rifles, no rules, no captain. Samir made his choice."

Ali's jaw clenched. "He trusted you."

That one hurt more than the blast.

"I know," I said. "That's why I carved his name into that train. So even if we die out here, something will still say he lived."

Ali turned to me, eyes wet but fierce. "Then we better make it to Benghazi. We better bring home enough metal to build a palace. Otherwise‚Äî"

"Otherwise he died for nothing," I said.

We sat there a long while, two shadows at the edge of the grave. Then I stood, brushed sand from my hands, and faced the others.

"Pack up," I said. "We leave now."

Ahmed looked up, startled. "Now? We haven't eaten."

"Then we'll eat in Benghazi," I said. My stomach was hollow but my voice was steel. "Samir's not here to walk with us anymore, so we walk for him."

Khalifa cursed under his breath, but he stood. Massoud followed. Ali wiped his face, slung his sack over his shoulder, and fell in line.

When I took the first step away from the grave, the sand crunched under my heel. It sounded like breaking bone.

I didn't look back.
